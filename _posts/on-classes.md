One thing that I've sort of thought about a lot is *how to choose classes*. I feel like most people fall under a couple of categories. 

- Pre-med 
- Life of the mind 
- Just getting by 
- Genius 
- Business Head

Also I will briefly talk about my own philosphy about choosing between "Honors" and "Regular" classes

## Pre-med (AKA GPA-Maxing)
These are the people who will go out of there way to take classes to maximize the following. They ethically do this by 

- Asking upperclassmen on which classes to take and avoid. 
- Reading course evalulations and reviews
- Taking it with friends 

They can unethically do this by 

- Getting previous years exams 
- **cheating**
- Waiting for certain professors to take that course. 

They have no remorse for what they actually want to learn, rather they only care about maintaining that *4.0* GPA or at least keeping close to that value. 

## Life of the mind

These people are people who will take classes that **truely** interests them. They really do not care what their resultant GPA is, rather they are interested in taking courses that truely interest them and will learn a lot. Symptons of this include 
 
- Skipping Pre-requsuite classes with instructor consent 
- When you ask them why they are taking a class, they can go on and on about it

## Just getting by 

These are people are interested in just graduating with the bare minimum. They will take the bare minimum number of classes required and take electives that seem interesting to them. They will stray away from "hard" classes unless it is required. There really isn't much more to say. 

## Genius 

They will take whatever class they want. They do what they want, and they will do extremely well. 

## Business Head 

These are people who intentionally take easy classes to take advantage of the extra time that is formed from it. Even then, the easy classes are those that derive some business value to it, I.E accounting, marketing, etc. Whether that be networking or understanding how a DCF works, they are doing something they a normal school education does not give them. 

## What that means

Ultimately, not everyone will fall under a certain category of individual listed here. In reality, many people are a blend of the above stereotypes. The important thing to note is that **there are benefits for taking behavior from each stereotype of individual**. Here are some examples on why this is true. 

- If your are an incoming freshman or a current freshman, asking upperclassmen about career paths and relevant courses to take are something that will benefit them in the long run. Doing so will save the freshman so much time and provide clarity on what they want to do 
- If preparing for recruiting for industry jobs and such, it is beneficial for anyone to network with people in that industry. If an individual were to recruit for an industry like investment banking, intentionally taking easier classes to reduce the burden of networking and interviews will make life so much easier. 
- Sometimes curbing your ego and doing the bare minimum is often the right choice. If you do not derive anything more from taking a harder course, then taking the bare minimum is more than enough. 

## On the decision between taking an "Honors" or a "Regular" course 

When I first entered undergad at UChicago, I thought it was really good to take extremely hard courses and take the hardest courses avaiable at all times. Whether that be the culture of the school I am in or just me as a person, that's how I viewed things. Now that I'm older, I have changed that philosphy a bit. After some time at UChicago, here is something that I ask myself before taking an Honors course:

- Do I derive anything additional from taking the honors version of this course?
- Will taking an Honors course be more relevant for the field that I want to be in (for example graduate school)?
- **Do I have time for this course and do I have the time management skills for this?**
- Am I at the level to handle this course?
- Am I certain the professor teaching the honors version of this course better than the one teaching the regular course?
- Is the expected grade in this course about the same as the expected grade as the regular course?

If the answer to most of the questions above is "Yes", you should probably take the honors version of the course. If the answer to most of the questions above are "No", you probably shouldn't take the course. Some of these criteria are more important than others, so consider carefully what you value the most.
